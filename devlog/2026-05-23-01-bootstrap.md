## What I set out to do

The repo had been running as an "8-hour LLM-from-scratch sprint" — eleven modules, timer per module, peek at solutions if you blew past 1.5× the estimate. That worked for what it was. But the bigger goal — landing at a frontier lab, demonstrating a specific skill the lab needs, lived out at the edges of the stack (kernels below, agent loops above) — needs a different framing entirely.

So this session was a **bootstrap**, not a teach. Per the operating charter at [`../MENTOR.md`](../MENTOR.md), I needed to: (1) research the current landscape so the resource library reflects 2026, not 2023; (2) honestly review what's already on disk (Modules 1–6); (3) decide finish-and-extend vs. restructure; (4) make the strategic call on the PyTorch→JAX bridge; (5) scaffold the new spine; (6) quiz cold to confirm the level.

## The wall I hit

The honest review of Modules 1–6 was uncomfortable. The prose in Modules 1, 4, 5, and 6 is genuinely strong — that's not the problem. The problem is that the *framing* of the whole course (8-hour sprint, "touch every concept") is exactly the wrong framing for the new goal (deep, evidence-driven, frontier-lab readiness). You can't sprint past a Chinchilla derivation. You can't sprint past identifying an unmodelled hardware constraint.

The temptation was option (a) — keep the sprint framing, just polish the gaps. It would have been a smaller change. But polishing within a frame that *actively rewards shallow touch-every-concept* would have steered every later phase the wrong way. So I had to throw out the framing while keeping the content, which felt like vandalism while it was happening.

Second wall: deciding the JAX bridge. Two reasonable positions tugged. Position A: JAX from day one, because Feinberg grades on JAX/Pallas — switching mid-course wastes a week relearning. Position B: PyTorch first, because PyTorch is unambiguously the best framework to *learn* in (dynamic graph, Pythonic debugging, the ecosystem most reading material assumes). I landed on B with a hard bridge at Phase 4 — Phases 0–3 are PyTorch (so the ideas can land without framework cognitive load), Phase 3 closes with a dedicated JAX primer, Phases 4–5 are where Pallas and the graded exercises live.

## The aha

The biggest realization wasn't technical — it was about evidence. The Module 1 PyTorch-parity test (`01_autograd/test.py`) is the single best pedagogical move in the whole repo. Not because it's clever, but because it *closes the loop*: "your from-scratch impl matches PyTorch to 1e-6." That assertion is the entire reason you believe your `Value` class is correct.

Every module needs this kind of closure. Module 4 attention should match PyTorch's `nn.MultiheadAttention` to 1e-5 with the same weights. Module 5 block should match `nn.TransformerEncoderLayer`. Module 3 BPE should round-trip identically. And from Phase 4 onward, the JAX impl should match the PyTorch impl. The parity test isn't decoration — it's what turns "I built this" into "this is the same thing the framework does, and now I understand what the framework is doing."

That single insight is what generalized into [`CONVENTIONS.md`](../CONVENTIONS.md). Every from-scratch numerical module gets a parity test. Every hardware-touching module gets a roofline. Every module gets a hand-math derivation. The discipline is the curriculum.
